The challenge

A brilliant project and a blank proposal are not the same thing.

Reviewers don’t fund the work you do — they fund the proposal in front of them. A weak narrative, a misaligned budget, or a missing Specific Aim can sink genuinely excellent science.

Writing to a federal solicitation is its own craft. It means answering the exact review criteria, in the structure reviewers expect, with the evidence they weight most — under a strict page and format limit.

How it works

A managed writing process, not a scramble.

01
Outline & criteria map

We reverse-engineer the solicitation into an outline mapped to every review criterion and scoring factor before drafting a word.

02
Drafting in passes

We draft section by section, reviewing with you at each pass so the science stays accurate and the argument stays sharp.

03
Budget & forms build

In parallel we build the budget, justification, and full forms package so nothing is left for the final week.

Representative engagement

Helix Therapeutics had the science. They didn’t have the proposal.

Representative engagement
HX
Helix Therapeutics
Immunology biotech · NIH R01 applicant
The situation

Helix, a small immunology team spun out of an academic lab, had compelling preclinical data for a novel autoimmune therapy and a looming NIH R01 deadline — but no one with the bandwidth or experience to write the application.

What we found

Their science was strong; their draft was a 40-page dump of methods with no narrative arc, a budget that didn’t match the aims, and three missing required documents. Reviewers would never reach the merit.

What we did

We restructured the application around the Specific Aims and review criteria, rewrote significance and approach for a study-section audience, rebuilt the budget to match the work, and assembled a complete, compliant package — handed off with a week to spare.
